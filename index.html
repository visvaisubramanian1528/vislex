A cross-border legal risk assistant. It helps screen transactions, search federal trade-risk databases, and review contracts for diligence issues.
Vislex is organized around structured legal workflows, such as deal screening, federal database search, and contract review Q&A. The output is formatted as a legal memo, not a conversation.
Attorneys, compliance teams, legal researchers, and students working on trade and transactional risk. The language and output are designed for legal analysis, not general business summaries.
It takes a deal description and returns a structured memo covering CFIUS exposure, sanctions concerns, tariff risk, government contract flags, and due diligence priorities.
Quick regulatory context from OFAC, CFIUS, USTR Section 301, Commerce AD/CVD, and BIS.
Uploaded contracts and agreements. It flags trade-risk clauses, missing protections, sanctions language, supply-chain terms, and gives attorney-focused recommendations as a practical issue list.
No. Vislex is a research prototype and decision-support tool. All outputs should be independently verified by a qualified attorney before use in any real transaction.
Most AI trade compliance tools help customs brokers classify HTS codes. Nothing was built for the M&A attorney doing cross-border due diligence — asking what trade risk needs to be surfaced before a deal closes. That is the space Vislex fills.
Harvey is broad legal AI, not trade-specific. Descartes does enterprise-scale sanctioned party screening. TariffLens serves customs brokers. None are built for the transactional lawyer screening deal-closing risk — CFIUS, AD/CVD, sanctions, government contracts — in one workflow.
A structured legal memo with a risk rating, deal stoppers, CFIUS analysis, a regulatory exposure grid, a due diligence checklist, and R&W flags — plus live federal database search and clause-level contract review, all formatted for attorney use.
